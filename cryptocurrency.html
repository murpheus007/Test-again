In late 2008, Bitcoin was launched as the first cryptocurrency and was intended to be the future of money. What is Cryptocurrency Trading?

Cryptocurrencies carry inherent value, and this has made them legitimate financial assets that can be bought and sold for profit. Based on this, cryptocurrency trading is the buying and selling of various coins or tokens with the aim of generating a profit. Investors can trade various cryptocurrencies via a crypto exchange or a CFD brokerage firm, such as Finnaprotrade.

When you trade via an exchange, you will need to create an exchange account as well as open a crypto wallet where you will be storing your coins. With an exchange, you own actual coins in digital form and must store them securely. You will generate a profit when the value of the underlying coin you are holding increases and you sell the coins at a higher price than that which you had initially bought for. If you sell at a price lower than the buying price, you incur losses.

In contrast, with a CFD brokerage like Vaultrise, you do not own the actual cryptocurrency. Instead, you speculate on price movements. Profits are made when your prediction is correct, and losses occur when it is not.

CFDs present an attractive method of trading volatile crypto markets, offering benefits such as leverage. Vaultrise enables you to trade top cryptocurrencies under competitive conditions, with low spreads and the flexibility to speculate on both rising and falling markets.

What Do Cryptocurrency Miners Do?

Cryptocurrencies operate like digital cash but are created through a process akin to gold mining. Crypto miners validate transactions on the blockchain, ensuring the security and integrity of the network. This process is known as mining.

As transactions occur globally, miners use computing power to verify and add them to the public ledger. Once confirmed, these records cannot be altered. In return, miners receive newly minted coins, introducing more cryptocurrency into circulation.

Many cryptocurrencies have a fixed supply. For instance, the total supply of Bitcoin is capped, with the last coin expected to be mined by the year 2140. This scarcity places demand as a key factor in determining price.

Bitcoin, the first and most prominent cryptocurrency, remains the benchmark for the industry. However, many others have gained significant adoption, including Ethereum, Ripple, Dash, Litecoin, NEO, EOS, Stellar Lumens, as well as forks like Bitcoin Cash and Bitcoin Gold.

Unlike traditional financial transactions, cryptocurrency exchanges do not require intermediaries such as banks or financial institutions. Every transaction is permanently recorded on a public digital ledger called the blockchain.

What is a Blockchain?

A blockchain is an open, digital, and distributed ledger that stores transaction records in a secure, transparent, and decentralized way. It acts as a public database not governed by any single entity. The blockchain is made up of numerous 'blocks', which are encrypted collections of transaction data linked sequentially.

When a new block is created, it is broadcast to the network. Users verify the block, and once validated, it becomes part of the blockchain. Each cryptocurrency has its own blockchain, powered by complex computer-generated algorithms. To interact with a blockchain, a user must have a cryptocurrency wallet (also called an e-wallet).

What is a Cryptocurrency Wallet?

A cryptocurrency wallet is a software or hardware tool that allows you to store, receive, and send digital currencies. Think of it as your personal crypto bank account. Most wallets are coin-specific‚ÄîBitcoin wallets handle only Bitcoin, while Ethereum wallets are exclusively for Ether.

Each wallet is encrypted and unique to its owner. When funds are sent, an encrypted message is broadcast to the recipient, which can only be decrypted by the recipient‚Äôs wallet. Crypto wallets come in two main forms: hardware (physical) and software (digital).

Why are Cryptos Ideal for Trading?

Cryptocurrencies have become a valuable component of modern investment portfolios. Their prices are largely influenced by demand and supply rather than traditional economic indicators or political events. In 2017, Bitcoin captured mainstream attention by surpassing the value of an ounce of gold.

This sparked institutional interest, prompted governments to explore digital currencies, and accelerated blockchain adoption. Cryptocurrency derivatives were introduced, expanding access and utility. During the 2020 global lockdown, people witnessed firsthand the flaws of fiat currencies as governments printed money to counter economic downturns.

As digital stores of value, cryptocurrencies have consistently outperformed traditional assets. Their appeal continues to grow, offering a new, high-volatility investment vehicle available for trading around the clock‚Äîeven on weekends.

Leveraged Cryptocurrency Trading

On Wall Street, most crashes have been triggered and worsened by leverage. The same applies to cryptocurrencies, where recent months have witnessed massive drops in nearly all coins and tokens‚Äîexcluding Stablecoins. Bitcoin fell from around $65,000 to $30,000 and struggled to reclaim the $40,000 level as of July 2021.

Ethereum similarly declined from around $4,400 to $1,700. Other tokens saw losses as steep as 80% within just weeks of hitting their all-time highs.

One of the major contributing factors to this decline was the widespread availability of leveraged crypto trading. Leverage allows investors to amplify gains, but also significantly magnifies losses‚Äîespecially in the highly volatile crypto market. While professionals may understand and manage these risks, retail traders often do not.

During the COVID-19 pandemic, many retail traders entered the market searching for new income streams. While institutions triggered the late-2020 and early-2021 bull run, retail traders helped drive it further with leveraged bets.

However, the same leverage also accelerated the crash. Today, leveraged crypto trading can drive trends, intensify volatility, and extend price cycles. For professional traders, this presents both opportunity and additional risk.

Understanding Key Factors Influencing Cryptocurrency Prices

  • Supply and Demand: Limited coin supply boosts scarcity and can push prices up.
  • Regulation: Government policies can either fuel or restrict crypto growth.
  • Utility: The real-world usability of a coin affects its value and longevity.
  • Exchange Listing: Listing on major exchanges increases visibility and access.
  • News and Media: Public sentiment driven by news can cause rapid price swings.
